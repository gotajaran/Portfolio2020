UX/UI

Green Shirt Studio

Training actors, improvisors, and performing artists since 2009.

Overview

A new class registration workflow, and revamping the brand.

The Client

Green Shirt Studio (GSS) provides training for actors, improvisors, and performing artists.

The Problem

Students expressed frustration with the class registration workflow and outdated design.

The Outcome

New internal payment gateway using WooCommerce, and a brand redesign.

My Role

Sole proprietor for design and development. Paired with client on content and business strategy.

My Team

Client provided feedback for ideas, wrote copy, and finalized content.

My Contribution

Research/testing, IA, wireframes, prototypes, UI, style guide, and WordPress development.

Summary

GSS was using the same Wordpress template since 2009.

Green Shirt Studio's mission has been to create a safe and inclusive space where people can try out or hone their acting skills. While their WordPress website had served them well since 2009, it was time to address some ongoing issues that have plagued them the past couple of years.

Years of feedback from students, and concerns from the staff had them concerned that their current design and registration process was deterring new students. Our goal was to simplify the class registration process, condense the site's information architecture, and update their brandâ€™s visual design.

First, this case study will go over the process of creating a new class registration process (problem #1), then it will transition into the process of redesigning the brand and UI (problem #2). Bear in mind that both processes occurred in tandem to one another.

Problem #1: Class Registration

We worked together to discover why this was a problem.

Early on, I gathered as much information about how registering for classes could be frustrating. Through a website audit and discussions with the GSS staff, I learned in the class registration workflow that to complete your transaction you had to exit the site to go to a PayPal gateway. Oftentimes, students wouldn't have a PayPal account or transactions wouldn't finalize for whatever reason, and the GSS staff would end up finalizing transactions manually on their end.

Class Labels & Business Strategy

Part of understanding the class registration workflow included shifting through their class catalog for labels. Inadvertently, creating this visual diagram helped the client visualize the gaps between class sessions, which helped formed their business strategy and model going forward.

Information Architecture & User Workflow

I facilitated both user interviews and an open card sort because we wanted to determine the most important pages that could help persuade a user to register for classes. The insight of the audit, interviews, and card sort test helped me narrow down the number of parent categories we needed for the site map. Additionally, we operated under the assumption that the new class registration workflow should work internally to avoid potential drop-off. This meant transitioning away from PayPal, and using a Stripe gateway instead.

Sketching Ideas

Since we knew what the site would look like on a high level, I wanted to sketch out different ideas on how we can structure content. The purpose of sketching was to help the client visualize where content will go, and understand what a potential user journey may look like.

Wireframes, Copy & Collaboration

We used low/mid-fidelity wireframes as means to collaborate. As I iterated, the client team wrote the copy. The purpose of the wireframes was to provide the client a visual reference on how copy would be organized.

Usability Testing

I facilitated a usability test with past and present students to validate the class registration concept, our copy, and verify any usability issues. Looking back, it was probably wasn't ideal to use copy as means to validate our brand concept. Even though I explicitly told users to that we were operating at a lower fidelity on purpose, I would still receive feedback on color, type, and button choices. Ultimately, we mostly validated the main task, and identified potential usability issues.

Problem #2: Brand Identity

To develop a tone for their brand identity, I explored Green Shirt's history and future.

In our brand brief, we explored how the Founder had cultivated an energized community over the years. With bigger brands like Second City, and iO West, what was Green Shirt's value proposition? As a baseline, we defined 4 key areas of brand that we wanted to cultivate.

Mission

To make high quality performing arts training accessible to everyone.

Vision

Create a world of courageous, vulnerable, empathetic people.

Core Values

Compassion, vulnerability, empathy, and courage.

Personality

Nurturing, safe, accessible environment dedicated to.

User Interviews

Interviewing past and present students helped us understand their needs and goals. Based on these interviews I created personas that clarified GSSâ€™s business goals.

Casual Actor

New to acting, no previous training, interested in pursuing acting as a hobby.

Aspiring Actor

New to acting, some previous training, interested in pursuing acting as a career.

Early Career Actor

Has acting experience, has less than 5 years of training, has been in a handful of productions.

Working Actor

Experienced actor, significant amount of training, wants more practice.

Competitor Analysis

Examining other Chicago acting studios gave us a better idea of our value proposition, and helped see the pro's and con's of their website IA, class registration process, and branding.

People

What I liked from the original site was the use of these studio pictures of students dressed in green. I felt that they captured the diversity and inclusivity that we strived for in our brand brief.

Color

While I wanted the student pictures to be the focus of the site, I felt that green could be used to tie things thematically. The primary green was great for large surfaces; pairing it with bold white type ensured it passed WCAG standards. The secondary green was bright enough to highlight important items like links or buttons.

Typography

Per GSS's request, I used their typeface Zona Pro. Since it's x-height was so tall, I felt that it was better suited for headers. I choose the 800 weight because it made it more rounded. Going higher would have eclipsed the bowls of the "o's," and I didn't want it to look more abstract.

Final Output

Leading up to launch, we had an issue with our visual builder.

With not a lot of time to code the front-end from the ground-up, we resorted to using another plugin to solve our issue. The tradeoff with the switch was the inability to customize WooCommerce (or shop) pages. This meant my high-fidelity designs couldn't be fully represented in the final output. We had to compromise. In the end, I could make subtle changes to the WooCommerce CSS, but unfortunately this added to a more bloated code.

Class Catalog Workflow

In addition to the student workflow, I also thought about the staff workflow that would go into maintaining the site. Beforehand, GSS would manually update their home and class pages. With our WooCommerce plugin, we created a workflow where all they need to do now is label a class by category and add a start/end date for each product. Then voila, classes reappear and disappear on their own based on their stipulated start/end dates.

Reflection

The biggest thing I learned from this project was the importance of collaboration.

As the sole proprietor of this project, I found myself in a flow where I would present work to the client, receive feedback, then rinse and repeat. While I was fortunate that the client fully trusted my decision-making, and wasn't particularly picky with my choices, I found it more helpful to work together than apart.

For example, later on in the project we would whiteboard some ideas for the UI. Doing this helped the client feel more ownership over the process, and excitement for the project. I wish I spent more time at the beginning setting up expectations on what a human-centered design process looks like and how they could benefit from it.

Additionally, I wish I advocated for the scope to include some time to test and iterate after deployment. This could have been a period to address any potential usability issues that may arise from launch.
